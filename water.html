Today's Water Cycle on Earth: A NASA Perspective

Water stands as the essential constituent for life on Earth. When observed from the vantage point of space, the Earth presents an impression of abundant water resources (2). Nevertheless, merely a fraction of the Earth's water is readily accessible for human requirements. The water cycle delineates the perpetual circulation of water across the Earth's surface, encompassing its presence in the atmosphere, on the surface, and beneath the ground (1).

The water content of the Earth totals approximately 370 quintillion gallons of water, with the predominant share, around 96.5%, residing within the global oceans (3). The remaining distribution comprises approximately 1.7% contained in polar ice caps, glaciers, and perpetual snow, and an additional 1.7% allocated to groundwater, lakes, rivers, streams, and soil. Merely a minute fraction, one-thousandth of 1%, manifests as water vapor within the Earth's atmosphere (1). That gives us 0.01% of freshwater that we can use.

Water is practically everywhere on Earth. Moreover, it is the only known substance that can naturally exist as a gas, a liquid, and a solid within the relatively small range of air temperatures and pressures found at the Earth’s surface (1). Sometimes, all three states are even present at the same time and place (1).

The hydrological cycle constitutes a dynamic and perpetual process, elucidating the continuous movement of water across the Earth's expanse. It comprehensively involves water's manifestation in the atmosphere, on the surface, and subsurface, encompassing the transitional phases between these states. Influencing the rate of evaporation at the ocean surface are factors such as sea surface temperature, surface winds, and air temperature (3). Notably, in tropical regions, elevated ocean surface temperatures contribute to heightened rates of evaporation. Wind also increases evaporation. When the air's temperature is warmer, it can hold more water (3).

Although the atmosphere doesn't contain a substantial volume of water in comparison to oceans, rivers, and lakes, it serves as a rapid means of water transportation between locations (2). Low-lying regions of the atmosphere with high moisture and strong winds can form "atmospheric rivers" to transport water horizontally (4).

Clouds are formed as water vapor cools and condenses into droplets and ice crystals. Clouds and water vapor act as insulators in the atmosphere. Clouds help shield the Earth from the Sun and trap heat from below (3)(4). When cloud particles grow large enough, they may fall out as rain or snow (2). Under the right conditions, areas of precipitation can grow into large storms.

As storms grow, they transfer heat vertically into the upper atmosphere (2). Water vapor—and with-it energy—is carried around the globe by weather systems (1). The presented satellite image illustrates the dispersion of water vapor across Africa and the Atlantic Ocean. Areas depicted in white indicate elevated concentrations of water vapor, whereas darker regions signify comparatively arid conditions (1).

In conclusion, understanding Earth's water cycle is crucial for life on our planet. It affects us all - from our daily weather to long-term climate patterns. As we continue to study this vital process with data from NASA's Earth-observing satellites, we gain a deeper understanding of our home planet.
